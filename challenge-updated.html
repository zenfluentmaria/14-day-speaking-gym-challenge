Five real tools from the IBS methodology: CEO Framework, CEO + Marching, Inbox Method, Predict the Scenario, Rapid Interview Rounds, and the Pre-Frame Framework. Week 1 introduces each tool with a specific topic. Week 2 applies them to real professional situations.

Day 1CEO Framework — "What does your team actually do?"+

The CEO Framework is: Core message (one sentence — what you want them to leave with) → Explain with one thing (one example, one data point, one story — not three) → Own the ending (a clear close that doesn't trail off into "so… yeah"). Today's topic sounds simple. It isn't. Most people explain their team's work in a rambling, context-heavy way that makes the other person work too hard to understand. Your job today is to make it effortless for the listener.

Your Task
  • Set a visible 90-second countdown clock in your recording. This is non-negotiable — the clock creates the pressure.
  • Use CEO to answer: "What does your team actually do, and why does it matter to the business?" Core message first. One explanation. Own the ending.
  • Record it once. No re-recording. The first take is the most honest version of where you are.

Day 3Inbox Method — "Can you update us on where the project stands?"+

The Inbox Method is about sorting what you're going to say before you say it — the way you'd sort emails by priority. You decide what goes first (most important), what goes second (supporting context), and what doesn't get sent at all (the detail no one asked for). The trap with project updates is that most people start from the beginning of the timeline instead of from what matters most right now. Today you break that habit.

Your Task
  • Think of a real project you could be asked to update someone on. Before recording, spend 2 minutes sorting: what is the most important thing right now? What context does that require? What can you cut entirely?
  • Record a 2-minute spoken update using that sorted structure — most important first, minimal context, clear close.
  • Then record a 60-second reflection: what did you cut that you would normally have included? Did cutting it make the answer weaker or stronger?

Day 6Pre-Frame Framework — "I'd like to share an update on [X] before we discuss anything else"+

The Pre-Frame gives you control of a conversation before it can go in a direction you're not ready for. The structure is: Set the direction (tell them what this conversation is about before they set the terms) → Set the ending (tell them what you want to have decided or agreed by the end) → Ask for agreement ("Does that work for you?"). Then you move into CEO for the substance. Today's scenario: you need to share a project update that includes a problem, and you want to control how it's received.

Your Task
  • Think of a real situation where you've had to share difficult news or a complex update — and either avoided it or went in without a frame and lost control of the conversation.
  • Record yourself delivering that situation using Pre-Frame + CEO. Set the direction, set the ending, ask for agreement — then deliver the update using CEO structure.
  • Record a 60-second reflection: what changed about how the message landed when you led with the frame?

Day 1Conditionals — "If I could redesign my morning, I would…"+

Day 3Passive voice — "It was expected. It was never really explained."+

Passive voice is a tool, not a mistake — when you use it intentionally, it shifts the focus from who did something to what happened. Day 12Hold the floor — present your view in a group without trailing off+

Holding the floor in a group conversation — making sure your point lands before you invite response — requires specific spoken behaviors: not inviting interruption mid-thought, not trailing off at the end of a sentence, not ending with "Does that make sense?"   • Phrases that signal you're not done yet: "Let me finish this thought…" / "I want to get to the main point before we discuss…" / "Hold on — I want to make sure this lands before we move on." / "Just one more thing on this…" — Say these calmly, not defensively. Volume and pace matter more than the words.
